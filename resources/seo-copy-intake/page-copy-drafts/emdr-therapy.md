EMDR therapy is one trauma-focused option. It can fit children, teens, and adults. It may help with memories or events that still feel stuck or too intense. Often, a client does not have to talk through every detail.

## When This May Help

EMDR may be worth asking about when a past event still shapes daily life. This may include:

- memories that feel hard to put in the past.
- strong reactions to reminders or triggers.
- avoiding people, places, or events.
- feeling tense, on edge, or quick to react.
- PTSD symptoms.
- the effects of childhood trauma, medical trauma, or past relationships.

EMDR is not right for everyone. Age alone does not decide fit. A therapist will look at the person's needs, goals, coping skills, and current level of stress.

## What Support Can Look Like

EMDR stands for eye movement desensitization and reprocessing. First, the therapist learns what is going on. You then choose what you want to work on. It may be a memory, thought, feeling, or body response.

The therapist may guide your eyes from side to side. They may use taps or sounds instead. This back-and-forth input is one part of EMDR. You notice what comes up. The therapist checks your pace and how you feel. You often do not need to say every detail out loud.

EMDR is not always the first step. Some clients need time to build trust. They may need coping skills or a sense of safety first. This early work is often called stabilization. EMDR may also bring up hard feelings, memories, dreams, or body responses. Symptoms can get worse for a short time. That is why plans and honest check-ins matter.

## EMDR, Trauma Therapy, And Your Main Therapist

[Trauma Therapy](/trauma-therapy/) is broad. It may use talk therapy, grounding skills, CBT, family support, EMDR, or other tools. EMDR is one method within that wider kind of support.

Some clients do EMDR with their main therapist. Others keep a primary therapist through [Individual Counseling](/individual-counseling/) and also meet with an EMDR-trained therapist. When this team approach fits, each therapist has a clear role. With your permission, they can work toward the same plan.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I have to talk through every detail during EMDR?

Often, no. You may need to name the memory or concern, but you may not need to retell the full story. Your therapist will explain what is needed.

### Is EMDR only for PTSD?

No. PTSD is one reason a person may ask about EMDR. It may also fit other memories or events that still feel stuck or too intense.

### Can children and teens receive EMDR?

Yes, when it is a good fit. The therapist will look at age, needs, coping skills, support, and readiness before making a plan.

### Can EMDR make symptoms worse?

Yes, symptoms may get worse for a short time. A therapist can discuss this risk, help you plan, and decide if other support should come first.
